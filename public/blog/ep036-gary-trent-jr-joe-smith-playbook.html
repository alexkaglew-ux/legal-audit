Gary Trent Jr.'s $64 Million Deal Is the Joe Smith Playbook, Replayed.

In 1999, the NBA stripped the Minnesota Timberwolves of five first-round draft picks over a secret side agreement tied to Joe Smith's contract. Gary Trent Jr.'s path to a $64 million deal with the Bucks follows the identical structural sequence — and the pattern, not the price tag, is what should draw scrutiny.

The Legal Mechanism

The Joe Smith case is one of the most cited cap-circumvention violations in NBA history. Minnesota signed Smith to a series of below-market, one-year deals rather than paying him at market rate — reportedly under a private understanding that a larger, backloaded payday would follow once cap room allowed for it. When the arrangement surfaced, the league didn't just void the eventual contract; it took five future first-round picks from the franchise and suspended the executive involved.

Gary Trent Jr.'s sequence follows the same shape without any suggestion of a hidden agreement: a minimum contract, then a below-market bridge deal, then a fully guaranteed $64 million contract that materialized the moment his Bird rights vested. To be clear, nothing here alleges Trent Jr.'s deal involved a secret side letter the way Smith's did — there's no public evidence of that. What's notable is that the sequence itself mirrors the exact structure the league has punished before, which is precisely why sequences like this draw front-office attention regardless of intent.

Why It Matters

Cap circumvention rules exist because the sequence — not the eventual number — is what the league can actually police. A team that structures a series of team-friendly deals immediately followed by a guarantee timed to a cap mechanism is running a play the league has seen before, whether or not anything improper is actually happening behind it.

For agents and front offices, that's the real lesson: the Joe Smith precedent means this exact shape of deal — minimum, bridge, guarantee-on-vesting — will always get a second look, deserved or not.

Episode Verdict

The structure matches a known violation. The pattern is the flag, not the price.

This is the clause I flag before a client signs.
