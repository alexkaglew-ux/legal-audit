Salary Cap EP037 · The Legal Audit

Wembanyama Gave Up $50 Million. Silver Calls That the System Working.

Victor Wembanyama left more than $50 million on the table, signing below his available supermax so San Antonio could stay further from the second luxury-tax apron. The players' union calls the apron mechanism broken. The commissioner calls it collectively bargained. Both things are true at once.

The Legal Mechanism

The NBA's second apron, introduced in the current collective bargaining agreement, layers escalating team-building restrictions on top of the luxury tax — limiting a team's ability to aggregate contracts in trades, use exception money, and eventually locking out entire tools like sign-and-trades once a franchise crosses the threshold. The rule doesn't distinguish how a team's most expensive contract was acquired. A max deal is a max deal against the apron math, whether the player was drafted and developed in-house or brought in via trade or free agency.

That's the mechanism Wembanyama's decision runs directly into. By signing for less than the supermax he'd earned, he's not avoiding a tax bill personally — he's giving the front office room to keep building around him without crossing a line that would cost the team its most flexible roster tools. The league's own defense of the rule is that it was negotiated by both sides at the table, which is accurate. It's also collectively bargained language that applies the same restriction to a homegrown foundation piece as it would to a purchased one.

Why It Matters

The tension here isn't procedural — nobody disputes the apron was properly bargained. It's substantive: a rule can be entirely legitimate in its formation and still produce an outcome that cuts against the stated goal of rewarding teams for developing their own stars rather than buying them.

When the price of keeping a homegrown superstar's supporting cast intact is the superstar himself taking a discount, the mechanism is functioning exactly as designed — it's just not obvious that "as designed" and "as intended" are the same thing here.

Episode Verdict

The CBA treats homegrown the same as purchased. That's the part Silver calls working.

This is the clause I flag before a client signs.
