Labor Law EP035 · The Legal Audit

Why Every Salary Cap Started With a Losing Union — and Why MLB Might Be Next.

The NFL, NBA, and NHL all have salary caps today for the same underlying reason: at some point, each players' union lost a fight and was forced into accepting one. MLB's union never has — and its current collective bargaining agreement expires December 1st, with a lockout expected almost immediately.

The Legal Mechanism

A salary cap isn't a negotiated convenience — historically, it's been a concession extracted from a losing position. The NFL's cap followed a decertification fight and a court-supervised settlement. The NBA's came out of lockouts where the league's leverage outlasted the union's. The NHL's arrived after canceling an entire season rather than operate without one. In every case, the union went into the fight opposing a cap and came out having accepted one.

The MLB Players Association has never lost that particular fight. It's one of the most successful labor organizations in American sports history, having preserved free agency and kept the league cap-free through every negotiation cycle to date. That track record is precisely why this December's expiration matters: it's the first time in a generation the league has signaled it intends to make this exact fight happen.

Why It Matters

This isn't a story about payroll numbers or which teams spend the most. It's a leverage question with a binary outcome: either MLB's union extends its unbeaten record on this specific issue, or it joins the other three leagues the same way they got there.

Whatever happens after December 1st will be decided by who blinks first in a lockout, not by which side has the more persuasive argument. That's worth knowing before the headlines start treating it as a debate about the game's economics.

Episode Verdict

Every cap came from a union losing. MLB's union has never lost — yet.

This is the clause I flag before a client signs.
